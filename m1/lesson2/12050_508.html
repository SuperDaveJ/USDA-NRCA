Slide Show Descriptions

Photo of Hugh Hammond Bennet helping a farmer in the Coon Creek Watershed.

In 1933, the Soil Erosion Service began working with farmers in the Coon Creek watershed of southwestern Wisconsin to transform the square, eroding fields.  With Raymond H. Davis, Bennet performed demonstrations of soil conservation techniques and provided farmers with seeds, equipment, and planning assistance.

Photo of gentlemen holding a conservation district sign.

The soil conservation work was made permanent in 1935 with the passage of PL74-46, which created the Soil Conservation Services (SCS) within USDA.  SCS expanded its work to blanket the countryside through conservation districts.  In 1937 and thereafter, conservation districts could be organized and sign cooperative agreements with USDA.  Eventually SCS established field offices where personnel worked with landowners in the nearly 3000 conservation districts.

Photo of NRCS worker assisting farmer.

In 1994, the SCS was renamed the Natural Resource Conservation Service (NRCS) to reflect the shift the service’s mission had taken to encompass soil, water, plants, and animals.
